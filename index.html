The Philippines in the 19th Century

Context of José Rizal

José Rizal was born in the 19th century, a time of major global transformation. The Industrial Revolution changed how people worked and lived, replacing manual labor with machines and creating growing cities. Political revolutions in America and France spread new ideas about freedom, equality, and human rights. This was the world José Rizal was born into in 1861, a world caught between old traditions and new possibilities.

Economic and Social Transformation in the Philippines

Before the Spanish came, Filipinos traded freely with neighboring countries such as China, Japan, Siam, Cambodia, India, Borneo, and the Moluccas. But Spain monopolized Manila's ports, creating the Galleon Trade also known as Manila-Acapulco Trade. In 1813, this system ended and Spain opened the Philippines to world commerce in 1834, turning the country into a supplier of raw materials for Western industries. When the Suez Canal opened in 1869, the journey between Europe and the Philippines became much shorter, allowing liberal ideas to flow in and wealthy Filipino families to send their sons to study in Europe. This created the ilustrado class, educated Filipinos who would lead the reform movement.

The Rise of Wealthy Filipino Families

The new cash-crop economy created a Filipino middle class. The Inquilino system allowed wealthy Filipinos to lease large estates from friars and profit from sugar, tobacco, and abaca production. Rizal's own family, the Mercados, became prosperous landowners in Laguna. This wealth gave them the means to educate their children well. But economic growth also widened the gap between rich and poor, sparking revolts like the Basi Revolt in Ilocos.

Conditions Under Spanish Rule

Despite all this economic progress, Spanish colonial rule remained harsh and unfair. Friars and Spanish officials controlled everything: politics, education, and the economy. They treated brown-skinned Filipinos as inferior, childlike, and incapable of self-government. Justice was expensive, slow, and biased. When harsh Governor-General Izquierdo took power, his brutal policies led to the Cavite Mutiny of 1872 and the execution of three Filipino priests, Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora (GomBurZa). These executions deeply affected the young Rizal and fueled the nationalist movement.

Educational Reform

The Industrial Revolution led to the need for changes in the Philippine education system so that it could focus on preparing skilled workers who could meet economic demands.

To address the shortage of formal education for Filipinos, the Spanish colonial government implemented the Educational Decree of 1863. This law required the creation of a nationwide public school system, mandating at least one primary school for boys and girls in every town. It marked the beginning of a more organized public education system and increased access to schooling for many Filipinos. Despite this, reports indicated that the poor condition of education in places like Pangasinan was not mainly because teachers were underpaid, but because there were too few teachers compared to the number of students. Whether a town had 250 or even 1,000 pupils, there were typically only one male and one female teacher assigned.

Europe's Crisis and Rizal's Education

Europe in the 1800s was going through an identity crisis. Industrialization made life feel mechanical and cold. Darwin's theory challenged the idea that humans were special creations. Different philosophies like Marxism, positivism, and romanticism competed for followers. Rizal, as an ilustrado studying in Europe, absorbed liberal ideas about equality and freedom. But he noticed something troubling, Europeans preached equality at home while treating their colonies and colonized people as inferior. This contradiction pushed Rizal to develop his own philosophy, one rooted in Filipino experience rather than blindly copying European thought.

Ideas and Awakening

The Ateneo Municipal de Manila influenced Filipino nationalism by shaping the intellectual, emotional, and patriotic development of José Rizal, who said his education there transformed him through discipline, study, and Jesuit guidance, while also strengthening his patriotic feelings and awareness. Although the school did not intend to teach nationalism, its humanistic training emphasized justice and human dignity, giving Rizal a foundation that later deepened when the Philippines opened to world trade and foreign ideas entered through books and printed materials. Through this exposure, Rizal encountered Enlightenment thinkers such as John Locke and Jean‑Jacques Rousseau, as well as European literary works like The Count of Monte Cristo and The Three Musketeers by Alexandre Dumas, which exposed him to themes of justice, freedom, and resistance. Seeing the contradiction between Spain’s liberal ideals and its actual treatment of Filipinos ultimately shaped his decision to pursue reform through writing rather than violence.

Fighting with the Pen

Through his novels Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, Rizal exposed the cruelty and hypocrisy of Spanish rule. He challenged racist claims that Filipinos were weak or uncivilized, arguing that Filipino suffering came from colonial abuse, not racial inferiority. He proved through his writing that Filipinos were capable of reason, dignity, and greatness. His work helped create the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino students abroad who advocated for peaceful reforms.

Legacy

Rizal founded La Liga Filipina in 1892, promoting unity and peaceful reform among Filipinos. But the Spanish saw him as dangerous. In 1896, they executed him by firing squad. Rizal went to his death with dignity, uncertain whether his sacrifice would succeed but committed to the cause anyway. His execution didn't end the movement, it ignited it. The Philippine Revolution followed, inspired by the ideas and courage of a man who fought with words instead of weapon.

The 19th century gave birth to modern Filipino nationalism, and José Rizal was its voice. The economic changes, educational opportunities, and exposure to liberal ideas awakened Filipinos to their own dignity and potential. Rizal transformed that awakening into a movement that would change Philippine history forever.

Timeline: The 19th Century as Rizal's Context

Early 1800s - End of the Galleon Trade
  • The Manila–Acapulco Galleon Trade officially ended.
  • Spain began shifting the Philippine economy toward world trade and export agriculture.
1834 - Opening of the Philippines to World Commerce
  • Manila and other ports were opened to foreign traders.
  • Cash crops like sugar, tobacco, and abaca expanded.
  • A Filipino middle class (local elites and inquilinos) began to rise.
1861 - Birth of José Rizal
  • Rizal was born into a relatively wealthy family in Calamba, Laguna.
  • His family's status allowed him access to education, which shaped his critical thinking.
1863 - Education Decree of 1863
  • Spain introduced a public education system.
  • Although limited and poorly implemented, it increased literacy and awareness among Filipinos.
  • Education became a key factor in shaping future reformists.
1868 - Liberal Reforms in Spain Reach the Philippines
  • Queen Isabella II was overthrown in Spain.
  • Liberal Governor-General Carlos María de la Torre introduced reforms and encouraged freedom of thought.
  • Filipinos experienced brief political openness.
1869 - Opening of the Suez Canal
  • Travel time between Europe and the Philippines was shortened.
  • More Filipinos, including ilustrados, studied in Europe.
  • European liberal and nationalist ideas spread faster to the Philippines.
1872 - Cavite Mutiny and Execution of GomBurZa
  • Filipino priests Gómez, Burgos, and Zamora were executed.
  • This event awakened Filipino nationalism.
  • Rizal later dedicated El Filibusterismo to GomBurZa.
1880s - Rise of the Ilustrados and the Propaganda Movement
  • Educated Filipinos in Europe demanded reforms, equality, and representation.
  • Rizal became actively involved through writing and advocacy.
1887 - Publication of Noli Me Tangere
  • Exposed abuses of friars and colonial officials.
  • Reflected real social conditions in 19th-century Philippine society.
1891 - Publication of El Filibusterismo
  • Darker and more radical than Noli Me Tangere.
  • Showed the consequences of continued oppression and failed reforms.
1892 - Founding of La Liga Filipina
  • Rizal promoted peaceful reform, unity, and education among Filipinos.
  • Spanish authorities saw it as a threat and exiled Rizal.
1896 - Execution of José Rizal
  • Rizal was executed in Bagumbayan.
  • His death intensified Filipino resistance and inspired the Philippine Revolution.
End of 19th Century - Rise of Filipino Nationalism
  • Rizal's ideas helped awaken national consciousness.
  • The century ended with Filipinos actively resisting Spanish rule.
